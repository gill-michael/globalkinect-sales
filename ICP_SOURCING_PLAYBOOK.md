## 1. THE SOURCING PRINCIPLE

The right account for GlobalKinect is not a company that might benefit from payroll software. It is a company whose specific compliance situation — in a specific country, at a specific headcount threshold, under a specific regulatory obligation — means they are experiencing a problem right now that GlobalKinect is uniquely positioned to resolve. Every sourcing decision starts from the compliance pressure, not from the software category. A company that crossed 50 employees in the UAE has an Emiratisation obligation that did not exist six months ago and carries a fine of AED 6,000-7,000 per month per unfilled position. A company that just signed a Saudi government contract under the Vision 2030 RHQ Programme must run KSA payroll for the first time — GOSI, Mudad, Nitaqat, EOSB — from day one. A company running payroll through separate bureaus in UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar has three different EOSB formulas accruing under three different rules with no unified view. These are not hypothetical needs. They are active compliance exposures with measurable consequences. Source from the compliance problem. Everything else follows.

### ICP A1 — Frustrated GCC Operator

**Who they are in sourcing terms**

From the outside, an A1 company looks like a mid-size business with employees on the ground in two or more GCC countries simultaneously. Their LinkedIn company page shows headcount between 50 and 500. They have job postings in multiple GCC locations — a Finance Manager in Dubai and an HR Coordinator in Riyadh at the same time. Their website or LinkedIn "About" section references UAE and Saudi Arabia (or Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman) as active operating countries, not just sales territories. They are in operationally heavy sectors: engineering, construction, hospitality, logistics, healthcare, retail, real estate, or professional services. Their HR and Finance leadership profiles on LinkedIn show mid-career operators — people with 5-15 years in GCC markets who understand the compliance burden from lived experience.

**Primary sourcing signals**

1. LinkedIn company page shows employees distributed across 2+ GCC countries with a total headcount of 50-500.
2. Active job postings for Finance Manager, HR Manager, Payroll Officer, or People Operations roles in at least two GCC locations simultaneously.
3. Company has a Dubai Trade Licence or ADGM/DIFC entity AND a Saudi CR registration — visible through DIFC/ADGM public registries, LinkedIn office locations, or corporate website footer.
4. Senior HR or Finance leadership on LinkedIn with titles including "Regional", "GCC", or "MENA" in the role description — signals multi-country operational responsibility.
5. Industry sector is operationally labour-intensive: construction, engineering, facilities management, hospitality, healthcare, logistics, or retail.
6. Company website lists office addresses in two or more GCC countries — not just sales contacts but physical offices with local phone numbers.
7. LinkedIn posts from the company or its HR/Finance leaders referencing month-end payroll, WPS, GOSI, or multi-country reporting challenges.
8. Company size is large enough for Emiratisation obligations (50+ UAE employees) and Nitaqat tracking (any Saudi headcount).

**Compliance pressure indicators**

*UAE-specific:*
- Company has 50+ UAE employees: Emiratisation obligation is active. Must achieve 2% annual increase in skilled Emirati roles. Non-compliance fine: AED 6,000-7,000 per month per unfilled position. This is a recurring monthly penalty, not a one-time fine.
- WPS compliance is mandatory for every private sector salary payment through MoHRE-approved financial institutions. Non-compliance triggers a visa ban for the business — not just a fine.
- EOSB accruing at 21 days basic salary per year (years 1-5) and 30 days per year (years 5+) across all UAE employees. If tracked in spreadsheets, the liability gap compounds invisibly.
- Fixed-term contracts mandatory since February 2022 labour law reform. Any legacy indefinite-term contracts are non-compliant.

*Saudi Arabia-specific:*
- GOSI contributions required for all employees: 11.75% employer and 9.75% employee for Saudi nationals; occupational hazard only (1-2% by industry) for expats.
- Mudad-compliant salary payment mandatory for all private sector employers. This is Saudi's WPS equivalent.
- Nitaqat band is calculated by headcount and sector. A company that grew headcount without adjusting their Saudi national ratio may have dropped from Green to Yellow or Red — restricting new visa issuance and government services access.
- EOSB at half month salary per year (years 1-5) and one month per year (years 5+), calculated on last drawn basic salary. Different formula from UAE.

*Multi-country:*
- EOSB accrues under different formulas in every GCC country. UAE uses 21/30 days on basic salary. Saudi uses half/full month on last drawn basic. Qatar uses 3 weeks. Kuwait uses 15 days/1 month. Bahrain uses half month. Oman uses 15 days/1 month with a break at year 3 instead of year 5. A company with employees in three GCC countries has three different EOSB accrual calculations running simultaneously. If managed in spreadsheets, errors are certain.
- Different WPS systems: UAE requires WPS file submission through MoHRE-approved channels. Saudi requires Mudad-compliant payments. Qatar has its own WPS. Each requires different file formats and compliance processes per pay cycle.

**Where to find them**

- *LinkedIn Sales Navigator:* Filter by company headcount 51-500, geography "United Arab Emirates" AND "Saudi Arabia" (or any two GCC countries), industry filters for Construction, Real Estate, Hospitality, Healthcare, Facilities Services, Transportation and Logistics. Search for decision makers with titles: HR Director, Finance Director, Head of Payroll, Chief People Officer, Regional HR Manager.
- *Job posting signals:* Search LinkedIn Jobs and Indeed Gulf for companies posting "Payroll Manager", "HR Manager", or "Finance Manager" roles in two or more GCC countries simultaneously. A company hiring payroll staff in multiple GCC locations is either scaling or replacing — both are buying signals.
- *News triggers:* Gulf News, Arabian Business, Zawya, and Argaam for companies announcing GCC expansion, new office openings, or contract wins requiring multi-country operations.
- *Industry directories:* Dubai Chamber of Commerce member directory, Saudi Council of Engineers registered companies, Abu Dhabi Chamber, Sharjah Chamber. Cross-reference membership in multiple chambers.
- *Public signals:* Companies listed in DIFC/ADGM registries that also show Saudi CR numbers on corporate filings. Free zone entity registrations visible through DED, JAFZA, or DAFZA public search tools.

**Disqualification signals**

- Company LinkedIn page shows GCC locations but all employees are actually based in a single country with a single entity — no multi-country payroll complexity exists.
- Company has fewer than 50 employees per GCC country — below the threshold where fragmentation becomes painful and below Emiratisation obligations in UAE.
- Company is a regional sales office with no local employees on GCC employment contracts — they have a commercial presence but not a payroll presence.
- Company operates entirely within a single free zone (DIFC, ADGM, JAFZA) with a single unified payroll structure — the multi-bureau fragmentation problem does not apply.
- Company has recently announced a switch to a direct competitor (Papaya Global, Deel, Remote, Oyster) — visible through press releases, LinkedIn announcements from HR leadership, or job postings referencing a specific platform.
- Company is public sector or government-owned. GlobalKinect serves private sector employers.
- Company headcount is above 500 and they have a dedicated in-house payroll operations team — they are unlikely to outsource to a platform without a lengthy enterprise sales cycle.

**ICP fit score**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 3 | Company operates in one GCC country with 50-100 employees. Single-country payroll need exists but multi-country fragmentation pain is absent. May be A2 instead. |
| 5 | Company operates in 2 GCC countries with 80-200 total employees. Some compliance exposure but limited evidence of active pain — no payroll job postings, no public signals of compliance difficulty. |
| 7 | Company operates in 2-3 GCC countries with 100-400 employees. Job postings for HR or Finance roles in multiple GCC locations. Industry is operationally heavy. Emiratisation threshold likely crossed in UAE. Decision maker identifiable on LinkedIn. |
| 9 | Company operates in 3+ GCC countries with 150-500 employees. Active payroll or HR job postings in multiple countries. Decision maker on LinkedIn with "Regional" or "GCC" in title. Industry is construction, healthcare, hospitality, or logistics. Evidence of compliance pressure: recent Nitaqat challenges, WPS references, or multi-entity EOSB complexity visible in job descriptions or LinkedIn activity. |

Pipeline entry threshold: 6 or above. Accounts scoring 9-10 are immediate priority outreach.

### ICP A2 — GCC Single-Country SME

**Who they are in sourcing terms**

An A2 company is a small business in a single GCC country — usually UAE, increasingly Saudi Arabia. From the outside, they look like a 10-100 employee company with one office location in one country. They do not have a dedicated HR function — the CEO, COO, or a Finance/Office Manager handles payroll. Their LinkedIn company page is often minimal. They are in any sector but commonly retail, F&B, services, tech startups, professional services, and agencies. The telltale sign is that they are growing — hiring actively, posting jobs on LinkedIn or Bayt.com, but their job postings never mention an HRIS, payroll platform, or HR technology of any kind. Payroll is still being done by an accountant or in Excel.

**Primary sourcing signals**

1. LinkedIn company page shows 11-100 employees, single GCC country location (Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, or Jeddah most commonly).
2. Company is actively hiring — 3+ open roles on LinkedIn Jobs in the past 90 days — but no HR or payroll-specific roles posted. This signals growth without HR infrastructure.
3. Company website is professional but makes no mention of an HRIS, payroll provider, or employee portal. No "Careers" page powered by an ATS.
4. CEO/COO or owner is the most senior person on LinkedIn — no HR Director or People Lead exists in the company's LinkedIn employee list.
5. Company is in a growth sector in the GCC: tech startups, F&B chains, retail, digital agencies, professional services firms, healthcare clinics.
6. Company has received funding in the past 12 months (visible on Crunchbase, MAGNiTT, or LinkedIn news) but is still small enough that HR infrastructure has not been built.
7. Free zone registration visible (DMCC, DAFZA, JAFZA, SAGIA) — indicates a formalised business entity that must comply with payroll regulations.

**Compliance pressure indicators**

*UAE-specific:*
- Company with 10-49 UAE employees: below the Emiratisation threshold but still required to run WPS-compliant payroll every cycle. If the accountant is handling WPS file generation manually, errors are likely.
- Company approaching 50 UAE employees: Emiratisation obligation is about to activate. The AED 6,000-7,000/month fine per unfilled position will arrive within months. They may not know this is coming.
- EOSB liability is growing for every employee. At 40 employees with an average tenure of 3 years, the accrued EOSB liability is substantial. If tracked in Excel or not tracked at all, the first resignation creates a surprise.
- Fixed-term contract compliance since February 2022: a company that has not updated employment contracts since the reform has non-compliant contracts for every employee.

*Saudi Arabia-specific:*
- GOSI registration is mandatory for all private sector employees. A company with 20 Saudi employees must manage GOSI contributions (different rates for nationals vs expats) and Mudad-compliant salary payments. An accountant handling this manually for a growing company will hit errors.
- Nitaqat applies from the first Saudi employee. A small company in a sector with high localisation quotas (e.g., retail) may already be in Yellow or Red band without realising it.

**Where to find them**

- *LinkedIn Sales Navigator:* Filter by company headcount 11-100, geography "United Arab Emirates" (or "Saudi Arabia"), industry broad (any sector). Search for decision makers with titles: CEO, COO, Founder, Managing Director, General Manager. The absence of HR leadership titles in the company confirms A2 fit.
- *Job posting signals:* Companies posting 3+ non-HR roles on LinkedIn Jobs or Bayt.com in the past 90 days but zero HR or payroll roles. Growth without infrastructure.
- *Startup databases:* MAGNiTT (GCC startup database) for recently funded UAE and Saudi startups with 10-80 employees. Crunchbase filtered by UAE/Saudi headquarters and Seed/Series A stage.
- *Free zone directories:* DMCC member directory, DAFZA company search, JAFZA business directory, DIFC public registry. Free zone companies are formalised entities with payroll obligations.
- *Industry events:* Attendee lists from GITEX (tech), Gulfood (F&B), Arab Health (healthcare), and sector-specific SME events in UAE. These are operators, not enterprise buyers.
- *News triggers:* Gulf News and Arabian Business SME features. Startup of the Week coverage. Companies featured for rapid growth in local media.

**Disqualification signals**

- Company has fewer than 10 employees — below the threshold where structured payroll becomes necessary. An accountant is sufficient and the cost sensitivity makes conversion unlikely.
- Company already has an HR Manager or Payroll Manager on LinkedIn — they have built HR infrastructure, which means they likely already have a provider or are A1/A3 instead.
- Company is a sole proprietorship or freelancer collective with no employment contracts — no payroll obligations exist.
- Company is headquartered outside the GCC with a small GCC sales office — they are B1 or B2, not A2.
- Company has visible job postings referencing a specific HRIS or payroll platform (BambooHR, Gusto, Bayzat, ZenHR) — they already have a solution in place.
- Company is in a sector with extremely low margins (labour supply, low-skill staffing) where PEPM pricing sensitivity makes conversion unlikely.

**ICP fit score**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 3 | Company exists in UAE/Saudi with 10-20 employees. No evidence of growth. No compliance pressure beyond basic WPS/GOSI. CEO may be satisfied with current accountant arrangement. |
| 5 | Company has 25-50 employees in one GCC country. Actively hiring. No HR function visible. Growth trajectory exists but no evidence of pain — no job description mentioning payroll challenges, no LinkedIn activity about compliance. |
| 7 | Company has 40-80 employees in UAE or Saudi. Hiring actively (5+ roles in 90 days). No HR leadership on LinkedIn. Recently funded or featured in growth coverage. Approaching or past Emiratisation threshold (UAE). EOSB liability accumulating without visible tracking. |
| 9 | Company has 60-100 employees in UAE or Saudi. Strong growth signals (funding, contract wins, rapid hiring). CEO/COO is the decision maker. No payroll or HR platform visible. Emiratisation obligation active (UAE 50+ threshold crossed). Industry has high Nitaqat quota requirements (Saudi). Company is at the breaking point where the manual approach will fail in the next 1-2 payroll cycles. |

Pipeline entry threshold: 6 or above. Accounts scoring 9-10 are immediate priority outreach.

### ICP A3 — Scaling GCC Business

**Who they are in sourcing terms**

An A3 company is a Saudi or UAE-headquartered business growing fast — often post-funding, winning government contracts, or expanding from a single country into multi-country GCC operations for the first time. From the outside, they look like a company with 50-300 employees that has hired aggressively in the past 6-12 months. Their LinkedIn headcount has grown 20%+ in the past year. They are posting jobs for operational and technical roles at scale. They may or may not have an HR function — if they do, it is a single person or a small team that was adequate at 30 employees but is overwhelmed at 150. Their industry is typically technology, fintech, hospitality, construction, healthcare, sport and entertainment, or logistics. Many are connected to Vision 2030 contracts or Saudi giga-projects.

**Primary sourcing signals**

1. LinkedIn company headcount shows 50-300 employees with 20%+ growth in the past 12 months (visible through LinkedIn Insights or the "X new employees" badge).
2. Company HQ is Riyadh, Jeddah, Dubai, or Abu Dhabi. Saudi-headquartered companies are highest priority for A3 due to Vision 2030 activity.
3. Funding announcement in the past 18 months — visible on MAGNiTT, Crunchbase, or LinkedIn news. Seed through Series C. The funding creates both the headcount growth and the need to formalise HR infrastructure.
4. Job postings at volume — 10+ open roles across multiple functions. The company is scaling the team, not replacing individuals.
5. Job postings for "HR Manager", "People Operations", or "Payroll" roles appearing for the first time — the company is building HR infrastructure from scratch because growth has forced it.
6. Company is in a Vision 2030-adjacent sector: technology, entertainment, sport, tourism, healthcare, fintech, construction, defence, logistics.
7. Company has won a government contract, been named as a supplier to a giga-project (NEOM, The Line, Qiddiya, Red Sea Global, Diriyah Gate), or been publicly listed as an RHQ Programme participant.
8. CEO or COO is active on LinkedIn, posting about growth milestones, hiring updates, or Saudi market opportunity — signals a founder-led company at inflection point.

**Compliance pressure indicators**

*Saudi Arabia-specific (primary market for A3):*
- Company has grown past 50 employees: Nitaqat band now applies stricter Saudi national ratio requirements. If the company was in Green band at 30 employees but grew to 80 without adjusting, they may have dropped to Yellow — restricting visa processing for the next wave of hires. Red band means a hiring freeze.
- GOSI complexity compounds with headcount: managing 11.75% employer / 9.75% employee contributions for nationals alongside 1-2% occupational hazard for expats across a growing, mixed workforce. At scale, manual calculation errors are inevitable.
- Mudad compliance is mandatory for every salary payment. A company that grew from 20 to 100 employees in a year likely outgrew whatever manual process they started with.
- Vision 2030 RHQ Programme: companies that won government contracts must establish a Riyadh HQ and run Saudi payroll for the first time. GOSI, Mudad, Nitaqat, EOSB — all from day one. Most do not have the infrastructure.
- EOSB accrual is growing rapidly with headcount. At 200 employees with an average tenure of 2 years, the accrued EOSB liability is significant and growing every month.

*UAE-specific:*
- Emiratisation threshold at 50 employees: a scaling company in UAE crosses this threshold mid-growth. The 2% annual increase requirement and AED 6,000-7,000/month fine per unfilled position arrives while the company is focused on operational scaling, not compliance.
- WPS compliance must be maintained as headcount grows. More employees means more WPS file complexity per cycle.

*Multi-country emergence:*
- A Saudi-headquartered company opening a Dubai office (or vice versa) now has two different EOSB formulas, two different WPS systems, two different social insurance regimes, and two different localisation programmes to manage simultaneously. The complexity doubles overnight.

**Where to find them**

- *LinkedIn Sales Navigator:* Filter by company headcount 51-500, geography "Saudi Arabia" or "United Arab Emirates", sort by headcount growth. Search for founders and C-suite with titles: CEO, COO, Founder, Managing Director. Filter by industry: Technology, Financial Services, Construction, Healthcare, Hospitality.
- *Startup and funding databases:* MAGNiTT filtered by Saudi Arabia and UAE, funding stage Seed through Series C, 50-300 employees. Crunchbase with same filters. Wamda and Arabnet coverage of GCC tech ecosystem.
- *Vision 2030 and giga-project signals:* Saudi Press Agency (SPA) for government contract announcements. MISA (Ministry of Investment Saudi Arabia) press releases for new entrants to the RHQ Programme. NEOM, Qiddiya, Red Sea Global, and Diriyah Gate supplier announcements.
- *Job posting signals:* LinkedIn Jobs filtered by Saudi Arabia, posted in last 30 days, company headcount 51-200. Look for companies with 10+ open roles — high-volume hiring is the strongest A3 signal. First-time "HR Manager" or "Payroll" postings are especially strong.
- *News triggers:* Argaam (Saudi financial news) for funding rounds and IPO activity. Arab News and Saudi Gazette for Vision 2030 project updates. Tech in Asia and Wamda for GCC startup scaling coverage.
- *Industry events:* LEAP (Saudi tech conference), Future Investment Initiative (FII), Biban Forum (Saudi SME event). Exhibitor and speaker lists are high-quality A3 sourcing material.

**Disqualification signals**

- Company headcount is stable or declining — no growth signal exists. A3 is defined by scaling, not by size alone.
- Company already has a mature HR function with an HR Director, Payroll Manager, and HRIS Administrator visible on LinkedIn — they have already built infrastructure and are likely evaluating replacements, not first-time buyers.
- Company is a government entity or public sector organisation. GlobalKinect serves private sector.
- Company's growth is entirely outside GCC (hiring in India, Pakistan, or Egypt for export services with no GCC-contract employees) — no GCC payroll compliance need exists.
- Company is a subsidiary of a large multinational that runs payroll through the parent company's global provider — the buying decision is not local.
- Company has fewer than 50 employees and is not demonstrably growing — they are A2, not A3.

**ICP fit score**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 3 | Company has 50-80 employees in Saudi or UAE. Some growth visible but slow. No funding signals. No Vision 2030 connection. HR function may already exist. Could be A1 or A2 instead. |
| 5 | Company has 80-150 employees. Growing (10-20% in past year). Based in Riyadh or Dubai. No first-time HR/Payroll job posting. CEO is active on LinkedIn. Industry is relevant but no specific compliance trigger identified. |
| 7 | Company has 100-250 employees. 20%+ headcount growth in past 12 months. Recent funding round or government contract visible. First HR/Payroll role posted. Saudi-headquartered in a Vision 2030 sector. Nitaqat pressure likely. CEO/COO identifiable and reachable on LinkedIn. |
| 9 | Company has 150-300 employees. 30%+ growth. Series A/B funding in past 12 months or named as a Vision 2030/giga-project supplier. First HR hire posted. Saudi-headquartered. CEO is the decision maker and active on LinkedIn. Multiple compliance pressures active simultaneously: Nitaqat band at risk, GOSI complexity at scale, Mudad compliance under strain, EOSB liability growing fast. No current payroll platform visible. |

Pipeline entry threshold: 6 or above. Accounts scoring 9-10 are immediate priority outreach.

### ICP B1 — UK/European Company Expanding to GCC

**Who they are in sourcing terms**

A B1 company is a UK or European-headquartered business that is either expanding into GCC markets for the first time or already operating there with an inadequate payroll setup. From the outside, they look like a company with 100-2,000 employees globally, with a GCC headcount of 10-200 or active plans to hire. Their LinkedIn company page shows a UK, German, Dutch, Swedish, or Irish HQ with a recently added Dubai or Riyadh office location. They may have job postings for GCC-based roles — often senior operational or project delivery roles — while their HR and Finance leadership remains in Europe. Their industry is commonly professional services, energy, construction, defence, technology, financial services, or healthcare. They are often using a global payroll provider (ADP, Papaya Global, Deel, CloudPay) that handles Europe adequately but treats GCC as a bolt-on with limited local compliance depth.

**Primary sourcing signals**

1. LinkedIn company page shows European HQ (UK, Germany, Netherlands, Sweden, Ireland, France) with a recently added office in Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, or Doha — visible through LinkedIn "Offices" section or job posting locations.
2. Company is posting job roles in GCC locations for the first time — project managers, engineers, consultants, or country managers in UAE or Saudi Arabia. This signals market entry.
3. Company has 100-2,000 global employees but GCC headcount appears small (10-50) — visible through LinkedIn employee search filtered by UAE/Saudi.
4. Press release or LinkedIn announcement about winning a GCC contract, establishing a GCC office, or entering the Saudi market. Saudi RHQ Programme participation is the strongest trigger.
5. HR Director, People Director, or Global Mobility Lead is based in Europe — signals that GCC payroll is being managed remotely, likely through an inadequate global provider.
6. Company LinkedIn page or website mentions "global" or "international" presence but the GCC office is new enough that it does not appear on the corporate website footer yet.
7. Job postings for a "Regional HR Manager — Middle East" or "GCC Country Manager" — signals the company is building GCC operational capability.
8. Company is in a sector that frequently wins GCC project contracts: engineering and construction, defence and security, energy, professional services, healthcare consulting.

**Compliance pressure indicators**

*Saudi Arabia-specific (most common B1 entry point):*
- Vision 2030 RHQ Programme: European companies bidding on Saudi government contracts must establish a Riyadh Regional Headquarters. This triggers a day-one requirement to run Saudi payroll — GOSI, Mudad, Nitaqat, EOSB — with no existing infrastructure. Most discover these requirements after the contract is signed.
- GOSI contributions differ fundamentally from any European social insurance system. Employer contribution of 11.75% for Saudi nationals alongside occupational hazard only for expats. European HR teams accustomed to uniform contribution rates per country are unprepared.
- Nitaqat Saudization quotas apply from the first Saudi employee. A European company entering Saudi Arabia with an all-expat workforce needs to plan Saudi national hiring immediately or face visa restrictions.
- Mudad-compliant salary payments are mandatory. European companies cannot pay Saudi employees through their UK or European payroll systems.

*UAE-specific:*
- No personal income tax (unfamiliar to European HR teams accustomed to PAYE/PRSI/social insurance deductions). Payroll calculations differ fundamentally.
- WPS compliance is mandatory from the first UAE employee. The salary payment must go through MoHRE-approved channels. A European company cannot add UAE employees to their existing European payroll run.
- EOSB accrual is unfamiliar to European employers — it does not exist in UK, Irish, Swedish, or most European employment law. A European company employing people in UAE for 5+ years accrues a significant liability under a formula they may not be tracking.
- Free zone vs mainland employment: European companies often set up in DIFC or ADGM without understanding that employment law differs between free zones and the UAE mainland.

*Cross-border:*
- The company now runs payroll under two fundamentally different regimes — European (income tax, social insurance, statutory sick pay, pension auto-enrolment) and GCC (no income tax, WPS/Mudad, EOSB, localisation quotas). No single European payroll provider handles both well.

**Where to find them**

- *LinkedIn Sales Navigator:* Filter by company headcount 101-5000, HQ geography "United Kingdom" OR "Germany" OR "Netherlands" OR "Sweden" OR "Ireland" OR "France", with employee search filtered by "United Arab Emirates" OR "Saudi Arabia" showing 1-200 results. Search for decision makers: HR Director, People Director, CFO, COO, Global Mobility Lead, VP People. The decision maker is in Europe, not GCC.
- *News triggers:* Monitor Saudi Gazette, Arab News, and SPA for European company names appearing in government contract awards, RHQ Programme announcements, or giga-project supplier lists. Financial Times and Reuters for "Saudi Arabia" + "contract" + company nationality keywords.
- *RHQ Programme tracking:* MISA publishes lists and press releases about companies joining the RHQ Programme. Companies on this list have an imminent Saudi payroll requirement.
- *Job posting signals:* LinkedIn Jobs showing European-headquartered companies posting roles in "Riyadh" or "Dubai" for the first time. Indeed Gulf and GulfTalent for the same. The first GCC job posting from a European company is a strong buying signal.
- *Industry events:* ADIPEC (energy), IDEX (defence), Arab Health (healthcare), Big 5 (construction) — European companies exhibiting or attending GCC industry events are often in pre-expansion or early expansion phase.
- *Government contract databases:* Saudi Etimad (government procurement), UAE government procurement portals. European companies winning GCC government contracts must establish local payroll immediately.

**Disqualification signals**

- Company has been operating in the GCC for 10+ years with 200+ GCC employees and a mature regional HR function — they are past the expansion phase and any platform switch is an enterprise replacement, not a first-time buy.
- Company's GCC presence is a sales office only with 1-3 business development people on visitor visas — no local employment contracts exist and no payroll obligations apply.
- Company has publicly announced a global payroll provider that explicitly covers GCC (e.g., a press release about rolling out ADP across all markets including Middle East). They have a solution in place.
- Company is entering GCC markets through an acquisition of a local business that already has payroll infrastructure — the acquired entity's existing setup covers the need.
- Company headcount in GCC is planned but zero employees have been hired — the need is pre-contractual and may not materialise.
- Company is expanding to GCC through an EOR provider already (Deel, Remote, Oyster) and has no plans for their own entity — no payroll platform need exists.

**ICP fit score**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 3 | European company with a GCC interest mentioned on their website or in press, but no employees in GCC yet. No specific contract or timeline. Speculative. |
| 5 | European company with 5-20 GCC employees. Basic GCC payroll running through a generic global provider or local accountant. No visible compliance pain. HR Director in Europe has not posted about GCC challenges. |
| 7 | European company with 20-100 GCC employees. HR Director or People Lead in Europe managing GCC payroll remotely. Job postings for GCC operational roles. Evidence of provider dissatisfaction (posting "Regional HR Manager" to bring GCC operations in-house). Industry aligned with GCC project work. |
| 9 | European company recently named in Saudi RHQ Programme or awarded a GCC government contract. 30-200 GCC employees or actively hiring. Global Mobility Lead or HR Director visible on LinkedIn and reachable. Company has posted GCC-specific roles in the last 90 days. Current global provider does not have deep GCC capability. Multiple compliance obligations active: GOSI, Mudad, Nitaqat (Saudi) or WPS, EOSB, Emiratisation (UAE). High contract value — likely to need platform across both European and GCC entities. |

Pipeline entry threshold: 6 or above. Accounts scoring 9-10 are immediate priority outreach.

### ICP B2 — Global HR at GCC-Adjacent Company

**Who they are in sourcing terms**

A B2 company is a business with operations in both Europe and GCC — the buyer who needs both sides of what GlobalKinect does. From the outside, they look like a company with offices in London and Dubai, or Stockholm and Riyadh, or Dublin and Abu Dhabi. Their LinkedIn company page shows 100-2,000 employees across European and GCC countries simultaneously. Unlike B1 (who is entering the GCC), B2 is already there and has been for some time. Their HR team manages two fundamentally different employment law regimes from one function. They have a UK PAYE payroll running alongside UAE WPS, or Swedish social insurance alongside Saudi GOSI. Their Global HR Director or VP People is the buyer — someone who feels the pain of running two completely separate payroll processes with no single view. The contract value is the highest of any ICP because the switching cost once GlobalKinect runs payroll across both their European and GCC entities is insurmountable.

**Primary sourcing signals**

1. LinkedIn company page shows offices in at least one European country AND at least one GCC country simultaneously, with employee counts visible in both regions.
2. Total headcount 100-2,000 with meaningful employee presence in both Europe and GCC (not a token sales office in one region).
3. Job postings in both European and GCC locations within the same 90-day period — signals active operations in both regions.
4. HR Director, VP People, or Global HR Business Partner on LinkedIn with a remit that explicitly covers both European and GCC entities — "UK, Ireland, UAE, and Saudi Arabia" in the role description.
5. Company is in a sector that operates across both geographies: engineering, professional services (law, accounting, consulting), financial services, energy, defence, healthcare, construction.
6. Company website footer or "About" page lists offices in London/Dublin/Stockholm AND Dubai/Riyadh/Doha — both European and GCC presence is established, not aspirational.
7. LinkedIn posts from HR leadership referencing the challenge of managing payroll or compliance across different regions, mentioning "consolidation" or "single view" as a goal.
8. Company has been in the GCC for 3+ years — they are past the entry phase and now experiencing the operational burden of running two separate payroll regimes long-term.

**Compliance pressure indicators**

*European-GCC contrast (the core pain):*
- UK PAYE: automatic deduction, employer NI, pension auto-enrolment (8% minimum contribution), statutory sick pay, national minimum wage, P11D reporting. Every UK payroll provider handles this.
- UAE: no income tax, WPS mandatory, EOSB accrual (21/30 days formula), Emiratisation (50+ threshold), GPSSA for nationals only. No UK payroll provider handles this well.
- The same company must run both systems every month. Different calculations, different government submissions, different compliance penalties, different accrual mechanisms. No unified view exists.

*Country-specific pressures compounding across regions:*
- UK payroll provider cannot generate UAE WPS files or calculate Saudi GOSI contributions. The company runs a separate bureau or manual process for GCC.
- GCC bureau cannot handle UK PAYE, PRSI (Ireland), or Swedish social insurance. The company runs a separate process for Europe.
- EOSB (GCC) does not exist in European employment law. The accrual is tracked separately — often in a spreadsheet — because the European payroll system has no concept of it.
- Localisation quotas (Emiratisation, Saudization, Omanisation, Bahrainisation) must be tracked per GCC entity. European HR systems do not have this functionality.
- Notice periods differ dramatically: 30 days standard in UAE, 90 days for senior roles in Saudi, 3 months in Kuwait vs statutory minimums in UK that vary by tenure. A unified HR view needs to reflect these differences per employee per country.

**Where to find them**

- *LinkedIn Sales Navigator:* Filter by company headcount 101-5000. Search for companies with office locations in BOTH a European country (UK, Ireland, Sweden, Germany, Netherlands, France, Cyprus) AND a GCC country (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar). Search for decision makers: VP People, Global HR Director, Head of Global Payroll, Chief People Officer, CFO. These titles signal centralised HR responsibility across regions.
- *Job posting signals:* Companies posting "Global Payroll Manager" or "International HR Business Partner" roles covering both European and GCC entities. Job descriptions mentioning "consolidation", "payroll transformation", or "global HRIS" are strong indicators.
- *News triggers:* Financial Times, Reuters, Bloomberg for companies with established European operations announcing GCC expansion or vice versa. Particularly: engineering firms winning GCC infrastructure contracts, professional services firms opening GCC offices, financial services companies establishing DIFC/ADGM entities alongside London operations.
- *Industry directories:* Cross-reference DIFC member directory with UK Companies House for companies registered in both jurisdictions. Cross-reference ADGM company search with Irish CRO. Swedish Bolagsverket with Saudi MISA registration announcements.
- *Conference signals:* Attendees at both European HR conferences (CIPD Annual Conference, HR Tech Europe) and GCC business events (ADIPEC, AIM Congress, GITEX) — a company present at both is likely a B2.

**Disqualification signals**

- Company has a GCC presence but it is staffed entirely by secondees from Europe on short-term assignments — no permanent GCC employment contracts exist.
- Company's European and GCC operations are run as completely independent entities with separate P&Ls and separate HR functions that do not report to the same person — no unified payroll mandate exists.
- Company has fewer than 20 employees in GCC — the GCC payroll burden is manageable through a local accountant and does not justify a platform investment.
- Company has already consolidated onto a global provider that genuinely covers both Europe and GCC (rare, but verifiable through press announcements or LinkedIn from the HR team).
- Company's GCC operations are winding down — contract ending, office closing, employees being repatriated.
- Company's GCC employees are all on EOR arrangements through an existing provider and the company has no plans for their own GCC entities.

## 5. WHAT NEVER ENTERS THE PIPELINE

The following account types are immediately disqualified regardless
of any apparent ICP match. No exceptions.

1. **Theoretical compliance need only.** The company might need GCC payroll someday, or is "considering" GCC expansion, but has no employees, no entity, no contract, and no timeline. Speculative accounts waste pipeline capacity.

2. **Fewer than 10 employees in any single GCC country.** Below this threshold, the compliance burden is manageable through an accountant. The PEPM economics do not justify outreach, and the company is unlikely to invest in a platform.

3. **Single free zone entity with no mainland or multi-country complexity.** A company operating entirely within DIFC, ADGM, or a single free zone with one payroll structure and one set of employment regulations does not experience the fragmentation problem that GlobalKinect solves.

4. **Already signed with a direct competitor.** Evidence: press release announcing a global payroll provider (ADP, Deel, Remote, Papaya Global, Oyster, CloudPay), LinkedIn post from HR leadership about completing a payroll platform implementation, job postings referencing a specific platform in the required skills. If the evidence is less than 12 months old, the account is disqualified. If older than 12 months, re-evaluate — contracts come up for renewal and dissatisfaction may have developed.

5. **Public sector entities.** Government bodies, sovereign wealth funds, state-owned enterprises, and government-linked entities. GlobalKinect serves private sector employers. Public sector procurement cycles, requirements, and compliance structures are fundamentally different.

6. **GCC presence is a sales office only.** The company has a commercial registration and 1-3 business development people in Dubai or Riyadh, but no local employees on GCC employment contracts. No payroll runs. No WPS/Mudad obligations. No EOSB accrual. No localisation requirements. No compliance problem to solve.

7. **Company whose primary market requires coverage outside GlobalKinect's native engine countries.** If the company's main GCC payroll need is in a country where GlobalKinect does not have native engine depth, the account should not enter the pipeline. Only present accounts where native engines in UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, and Egypt cover the primary need.

8. **Companies with fewer than 2 GCC countries for ICP A1.** A company with employees in only one GCC country is A2 or A3, not A1. The A1 pain is multi-country fragmentation. One country does not produce it.

9. **Companies with no identifiable decision maker.** If the person who makes the buying decision cannot be identified on LinkedIn or through public sources, the account stays in discovery until a decision maker is found. Do not outreach to generic company inboxes or irrelevant contacts.

10. **Companies in active legal or financial distress.** Visible signs: layoff announcements, bankruptcy filings, litigation disclosure, office closures. These companies are unlikely to invest in a new platform and may create bad-debt risk.
